JR Southard :: Tooth and Nail #3 (Pennsylvania/Kentucky)

This work is presented as a part of JR Southard's residency on our Instagram feed. Originally created in 2011,"Tooth and Nail #3 (Pennsylvania/Kentucky)" is a digital photographic image.

* * *

The work is loosely based on the Lamentation of Christ motif which is incredibly important in the context of Western Art History. It became one of the dominant ways to visually express grief in a public setting.

Giotto, ”Lamentation of Christ”, fresco, early 1300’s

In fact, this motif influenced the way we portrayed historically significant moments of grief for public consumption. For example, by the end of the 18th century, we have historical paintings that echo the earlier religious compositions in a secular setting.

Benjamin West, “The Death of General Wolfe”, oil on canvas, 1770

Tooth and Nail #3 (Pennsylvania/Kentucky) is meticulously composited together from a number of images taken during multiple carefully staged photo shoots. This, of course, is a rather prolonged process but it allows for some very unique visual games with lighting. These have a close affinity to the way paintings have been able to deal with the light in the past (check out this Wikipedia entry on chiaroscuro technique).

Francisco Zurbaran, “The Lying-in-State of St. Bonaventura”, oil on canvas, 1629

* * *

From photoshoot in artist's studio

The individuals in the image are mainly artist’s friends from Louisville, Kentucky. The setting for this work also hails from this city. It is his mother’s living room. A place he can relate to on a very personal level.

Artist's mother's living room, Louisville, Kentucky USA

The objects the participants are holding are ad hoc weapons playfully cobbled together from materials kids would play with in a garage or a basement. As the composition took shape JR invited more of his friends from Pittsburgh into the shot in order to create an increasingly complex image. In the end his personal relationships with his subjects, as well as their relationship to each other, allowed him to create this beautiful dialog with the conventions of representation present in our culture. It is a way to take on the immense traditions of Western Art History and make them tangible, personal and accessible.
